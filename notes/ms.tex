\title{A Unified Bayesian Framework for Non-Stationary Power Spectral
Density Estimation from Time--Frequency Data with Tensor-Product P-Splines}

\author{Author Name}
\affiliation{Affiliation}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
We introduce a unified Bayesian framework for estimating smoothly
time-varying power spectral densities (PSDs) from time--frequency data. The
core of the framework is a single statistical model: the log-PSD surface
$\log S(t,f)$ is represented by a tensor-product P-spline with an anisotropic
roughness prior, sampled in a non-centered (whitened) parameterization that
removes the hierarchical funnel obstructing Hamiltonian Monte Carlo. The key
design principle is that this model is \emph{independent of the underlying
time--frequency representation}: the same prior and the same smooth surface can
be combined with different observation models. We develop two such likelihoods
-- one for moving-periodogram ordinates and one for Wilson--Daubechies--Meyer
(WDM) wavelet coefficients -- and show that they plug into the identical PSD
model. In a simulation study on a locally stationary process with known truth,
both observation models recover the time-varying surface with near-nominal
credible intervals across a range of observation lengths. We then demonstrate
the framework on a realistic LISA inference problem with a cyclostationary
Galactic confusion foreground -- modulated by the published annual law of
Digman \& Cornish -- and an injected Galactic binary, where a blocked-Gibbs
scheme alternates between PSD estimation and source parameter estimation, and
show that assuming stationarity biases the recovered noise PSD while the
non-stationary fit tracks the annual modulation and recovers the source. The
result is a modular framework, applicable to any
time--frequency representation whose coefficients admit a local-power
likelihood.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Classical spectral analysis rests on the assumption that the process under study
is stationary. For a stationary series the periodogram ordinates are
asymptotically independent and exponentially distributed about the power spectral
density (PSD), which yields the Whittle likelihood
\citep{whittle1953analysis,pawitan1994nonparametric}
\begin{equation}
L_{\mathrm{W}}(S) = \prod_{k}
\frac{1}{S(f_k)}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{I(f_k)}{S(f_k)}\right),
\label{eq:whittle}
\end{equation}
with $I(f_k)$ the periodogram at Fourier frequency $f_k$. Modern Bayesian
nonparametric estimators build on this likelihood by placing a smoothing prior on
the log-spectrum -- for example a penalized spline (P-spline) with a roughness
penalty \citep{maturana2021bayesian} -- so that a single noisy realization yields
a regularized estimate of $S(f)$ with calibrated uncertainty. This construction
is the starting point that the present work generalizes to the non-stationary,
time--frequency setting.

Real instruments rarely satisfy the stationarity assumption. Detector noise
drifts, glitches, and responds to environmental and instrumental conditions that
change on timescales short compared with a typical observation, so that its
spectral content evolves in time. Treating such noise as stationary
mis-specifies the very likelihood, Eq.~\eqref{eq:whittle}, on which downstream
inference depends, and the resulting bias propagates into any astrophysical
parameters estimated against that noise model.

The Laser Interferometer Space Antenna (LISA) is an acute example. Its dominant
low-frequency foreground -- the unresolved Galactic confusion noise -- is
non-stationary by construction: as the constellation orbits the Sun, its
antenna pattern sweeps across the anisotropic Galactic source distribution,
modulating the confusion power with an annual period. This makes the foreground a
\emph{cyclostationary} process, well described by a frequency-independent
amplitude modulation of a fixed spectral shape
\citep{digman2022cyclostationary, buscicchio2024test}, and recent stochastic and
global-fit pipelines model it by fitting the confusion amplitude independently in
short time segments \citep{rosati2024prototype, pozzoli2024bahamas}. The
foreground is simultaneously entangled with the resolvable sources one wishes to
characterize, so the noise PSD and the source parameters must ultimately be
inferred together \citep{katz2025efficient}. In this setting a stationary PSD
assumption is not a mild approximation but a structural mis-specification, which
makes LISA a natural proving ground for non-stationary spectral estimation.

A non-stationary process is most naturally described in a joint time--frequency
representation. Two complementary choices are the moving, or short-time,
periodogram, which slides a window along the series, and critically-sampled
wavelet transforms such as the Wilson--Daubechies--Meyer (WDM) transform
\citep{cornish2020Wavelets}, which tile the plane into a near-orthogonal grid.
The two produce different data products, but both estimate the same underlying
object: a PSD that evolves slowly across the time--frequency plane. When that
evolution is smooth, recovering it becomes a bivariate regression problem -- fit
a smooth surface to noisy local-power observations -- to which penalized splines
are well suited.

This paper makes three contributions. First, we introduce a Bayesian
tensor-product P-spline model for a smooth time-varying log-PSD surface
$\log S(t,f)$, with an anisotropic roughness prior and a whitened
parameterization that yields stable Hamiltonian Monte Carlo
(Sec.~\ref{sec:model}). Second, we develop two observation models that connect
distinct time--frequency data products to the \emph{same} PSD model: a
moving-periodogram likelihood and a WDM coefficient likelihood
(Sec.~\ref{sec:obs}). Third, we demonstrate that the framework improves inference
in a realistic gravitational-wave application, using simulated LISA data with
evolving Galactic confusion noise and an injected binary, where assuming
stationarity biases both the noise and source estimates (Sec.~\ref{sec:lisa}).

\section{Bayesian model}
\label{sec:model}

This section defines the statistical model for the time-varying PSD. It is
stated \emph{independently of the data representation}: the surface, prior, and
inference machinery below are shared by every observation model in
Sec.~\ref{sec:obs}.

\subsection{Time-varying PSD model}
\label{sec:tvpsd}

Let $S(t,f) > 0$ denote the power spectral density at time $t$ and frequency
$f$, assumed to vary smoothly in both arguments. We model its logarithm,
\begin{equation}
\Lambda(t,f) \;=\; \log S(t,f),
\end{equation}
working on the log scale so that the surface is unconstrained and the
multiplicative dynamic range of the PSD becomes additive. The estimand is the
function $\Lambda(t,f)$ evaluated on a time--frequency grid; the specific grid
$(t_n, f_m)$ is supplied by the chosen representation in Sec.~\ref{sec:obs}, but
the model below does not depend on how that grid arises.

\subsection{Tensor-product P-spline representation}
\label{sec:pspline}

We represent the log-PSD surface as a tensor-product P-spline
\citep{eilers2003multivariate, wood2017generalized},
\begin{equation}
\Lambda(t_i, f_j) = \sum_{k=1}^{K_t}\sum_{l=1}^{K_f} w_{kl}\,
B_k^{(t)}(t_i)\, B_l^{(f)}(f_j),
\end{equation}
where $B_k^{(t)}$ and $B_l^{(f)}$ are B-spline bases of degree $d_t, d_f$ in
time and frequency, $K_t, K_f$ are the corresponding basis dimensions, and
$w_{kl}$ are the spline coefficients. In matrix form, with
$\bm{\Lambda}\in\mathbb{R}^{n_t\times n_f}$ the surface on the grid,
$\mathbf{B}_t\in\mathbb{R}^{n_t\times K_t}$, $\mathbf{B}_f\in\mathbb{R}^{n_f\times K_f}$
the basis matrices, and $\mathbf{W}\in\mathbb{R}^{K_t\times K_f}$ the coefficient
matrix,
\begin{equation}
\bm{\Lambda} = \mathbf{B}_t\,\mathbf{W}\,\mathbf{B}_f^\top .
\end{equation}
Equivalently, with $\mathbf{w} = \mathrm{vec}(\mathbf{W})$,
$\mathrm{vec}(\bm{\Lambda}) = (\mathbf{B}_f \otimes \mathbf{B}_t)\,\mathbf{w}$
\citep{boer2023tensor}; in practice we never form the Kronecker design and
evaluate the dense product directly.

Smoothness is imposed by difference penalties in each direction. With
$\mathbf{P}_t$ and $\mathbf{P}_f$ the marginal difference-penalty matrices
(orders $m_t, m_f$), the anisotropic penalty on $\mathbf{w}$ has the
Kronecker-sum structure \citep{wood2017generalized}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Q}(\phi_t,\phi_f)
= \phi_t\,(\mathbf{I}_{K_f}\otimes\mathbf{P}_t)
+ \phi_f\,(\mathbf{P}_f\otimes\mathbf{I}_{K_t}),
\label{eq:penalty}
\end{equation}
with $\phi_t, \phi_f > 0$ the time and frequency smoothing precisions. The
penalty defines a Gaussian roughness prior on the coefficients,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{w}\mid\phi_t,\phi_f \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mathbf{0},\,\mathbf{Q}^{-1}\right),
\label{eq:prior}
\end{equation}
so that all smoothing information is carried by the two penalties and the two
precisions \citep{lang2004bayesian,maturana2021bayesian,boer2023tensor}.

Sampling $\mathbf{w}$ jointly with $(\phi_t,\phi_f)$ produces the classic
hierarchical funnel: the conditional scale of $\mathbf{w}$ collapses as the
precisions grow, stalling gradient-based MCMC. We remove it by sampling in the
penalty eigenbasis. With $\mathbf{P}_t = \mathbf{U}_t\,\mathrm{diag}(\lambda^t)\,
\mathbf{U}_t^\top$ and $\mathbf{P}_f = \mathbf{U}_f\,\mathrm{diag}(\lambda^f)\,
\mathbf{U}_f^\top$, the Kronecker terms are simultaneously diagonalized by
$\mathbf{U}_f\otimes\mathbf{U}_t$, so $\mathbf{Q}$ has eigenvalues
$d_{ab} = \phi_t\lambda^t_a + \phi_f\lambda^f_b$. We sample standard-normal
$s_{ab}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and set
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{W} = \mathbf{U}_t\,\mathbf{Z}\,\mathbf{U}_f^\top,
\qquad
Z_{ab} = \frac{s_{ab}}{\sqrt{d_{ab}}},
\label{eq:whitening}
\end{equation}
which reproduces $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{Q}^{-1})$ exactly while making
$\mathbf{s}$ \emph{a priori} independent of $(\phi_t,\phi_f)$ and absorbing the
$|\mathbf{Q}|^{1/2}$ normalization. The shared penalty null space (bilinear
trends in $t,f$, $d_{ab}=0$) carries no smoothing information and is given a
fixed weak precision $\tau_0$.

Each smoothing precision is given a Gamma hyperprior
\citep{maturana2021bayesian},
\begin{equation}
\phi_t,\ \phi_f \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha_\phi, \beta_\phi),
\end{equation}
sampled on the unconstrained log scale, with the Gamma density and its
log-Jacobian supplied as a factor. The weakly informative defaults place the
prior mass over the range of smoothing scales spanned by the basis, in the spirit
of penalized-complexity priors \citep{simpson2017penalising,yue2012priors}.

\subsection{Posterior inference}
\label{sec:inference}

Let $\mathcal{L}(\bm{\Lambda})$ denote the observation-model log-likelihood
(Sec.~\ref{sec:obs}), which depends on the data only through the surface
$\bm{\Lambda}$. The joint posterior over the whitened eigen-coefficients and the
smoothing precisions is
\begin{equation}
p(\mathbf{s}, \phi_t, \phi_f \mid \text{data})
\;\propto\;
\exp\!\big(\mathcal{L}(\bm{\Lambda}(\mathbf{s},\phi_t,\phi_f))\big)\,
p(\mathbf{s})\,p(\phi_t)\,p(\phi_f),
\end{equation}
with $p(\mathbf{s}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I})$ and $\bm{\Lambda}$
obtained from $(\mathbf{s},\phi_t,\phi_f)$ through the deterministic whitening
map of Eq.~\eqref{eq:whitening}; no explicit penalty factor appears. We draw
samples with the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS), using standard step-size and
mass-matrix adaptation during warmup. Each chain is initialized from a penalized
least-squares fit of $\bm{\Lambda}$ to the log of the local-power data, mapped
into the whitened coordinates; the WDM and moving-periodogram front ends use the
same warm-start procedure on their respective local-power statistics.
Figure~\ref{alg:psd} summarizes a single likelihood evaluation.

\begin{figure}[t]
\begin{algorithmic}
\STATE \textbf{Sample smoothing precisions (log scale):}
$\log\phi_t,\ \log\phi_f$, with $\phi_t,\phi_f\sim\mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha_\phi,\beta_\phi)$
\STATE \textbf{Sample whitened eigen-coefficients:} $s_{ab}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$
\STATE $d_{ab} = \phi_t\lambda^t_a + \phi_f\lambda^f_b$;\quad
       $Z_{ab} = s_{ab}/\sqrt{d_{ab}}$ \COMMENT{$\tau_0$ on the null space}
\STATE \textbf{Compute log-spline surface:}
$\bm{\Lambda} = (\mathbf{B}_t\mathbf{U}_t)\,\mathbf{Z}\,(\mathbf{B}_f\mathbf{U}_f)^\top$
\STATE \textbf{Evaluate observation-model log-likelihood:}
$\mathcal{L}(\bm{\Lambda})$ \COMMENT{moving periodogram or WDM; Sec.~\ref{sec:obs}}
\end{algorithmic}
\caption{Algorithm: whitened tensor-product log-P-spline PSD model (one
likelihood evaluation).}
\label{alg:psd}
\end{figure}

\section{Observation models}
\label{sec:obs}

The model of Sec.~\ref{sec:model} specifies a prior over the smooth surface
$\bm{\Lambda} = \log\mathbf{S}$. To complete the posterior we need a likelihood
that links a time--frequency data product to this surface. This section gives
two such likelihoods. The Bayesian PSD model is unchanged in both cases; only the
likelihood $\mathcal{L}(\bm{\Lambda})$ differs.

\subsection{Moving periodogram likelihood}
\label{sec:movperiod}

A short window of length $2m+1$ is slid along the series and a local periodogram
is formed at each step \citep{tang2023MovingPSD}.

\begin{definition}[Moving periodogram ordinates]
The \emph{moving periodogram ordinates} $\mathrm{MI}_t$ (of order $m$) at time
point $t = 1,\ldots,T$ of $X_{t-m+1},\ldots,X_{t+m}$ are
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{MI}_t = \frac{1}{2\pi(2m+1)}
\left|\sum_{\nu=0}^{2m} X_{\nu+t-m}\,
\exp\!\left(-i\pi\nu\,\lambda_{\mathrm{mod}(t)}\right)\right|^2,
\label{eq:MI}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_j = \tfrac{2j}{2m+1}$, $j=1,\ldots,m$, are the Fourier
frequencies and $\mathrm{mod}(t) = 1 + ((t-1)\bmod m)$.
\end{definition}

For a locally stationary process the moving periodogram ordinates are
approximately independent and exponentially distributed with mean $S$, giving the
dynamic Whittle likelihood.

\begin{definition}[Dynamic Whittle likelihood]
\begin{equation}
L^{(1)}_{\mathrm{DW}}(S)
= \prod_{t=1}^{T}
\frac{1}{S(t/T,\lambda_{\mathrm{mod}(t)})}
\exp\!\left(-\frac{\mathrm{MI}_t}{S(t/T,\lambda_{\mathrm{mod}(t)})}\right).
\label{eq:dynamic_whittle}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

To reduce the dependence induced by overlapping windows, a thinned variant
retains a zigzag subset of ordinates \citep{tang2023MovingPSD}.

\begin{definition}[Thinned dynamic Whittle likelihood]
With thinning factor $i$,
\begin{equation}
L^{(i)}_{\mathrm{DW}}(S)
= \prod_{l=1}^{B_i}\prod_{j=1}^{m}
\frac{1}{S(u_{j,l,i},\lambda_j)}
\exp\!\left(-\frac{\mathrm{MI}(u_{j,l,i},\lambda_j)}{S(u_{j,l,i},\lambda_j)}\right),
\label{eq:thinned_dynamic_whittle}
\end{equation}
where $B_i = \lfloor (T-m)/(im)\rfloor$ and $u_{j,l,i} = (i(l-1)m+j)/T$.
\end{definition}

In the notation of Sec.~\ref{sec:model}, $\mathcal{L}(\bm{\Lambda}) =
\log L^{(i)}_{\mathrm{DW}}$ with $S = e^{\Lambda}$ evaluated at the ordinate
locations. The dynamic Whittle treats the (thinned) ordinates as independent and
exponential; this is exact only asymptotically and requires the spectrum to be
approximately constant across a window. Thinning trades data for reduced
inter-ordinate correlation, and the power periodogram discards phase.

\subsection{WDM likelihood}
\label{sec:wdm}

The Wilson--Daubechies--Meyer (WDM) transform \citep{cornish2020Wavelets} tiles
the data into a near-orthogonal time--frequency grid of coefficients $w_{nm}$,
where $n$ indexes WDM time bins and $m$ frequency channels. For a locally
stationary process the diagonal approximation models each pixel as a zero-mean
Gaussian whose variance is the local evolutionary power,
\begin{equation}
w_{nm}\sim\mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\,S_{nm}\right),
\qquad S_{nm} = S(t_n, f_m).
\end{equation}
The resulting log-likelihood, with $\bm{\Lambda} = \log\mathbf{S}$, is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(\bm{\Lambda})
= -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{n,m}
\left[\log(2\pi) + \Lambda_{nm} + w_{nm}^2\,e^{-\Lambda_{nm}}\right].
\label{eq:wdm_like}
\end{equation}
Each pixel contributes a single $\chi^2_1$ observation; the smoothing prior of
Sec.~\ref{sec:pspline} supplies the regularization that makes single-realization
estimation feasible. The likelihood treats the WDM coefficients as independent,
which is exact only for a perfectly orthogonal transform; residual
nearest-neighbour time correlation remains, and as with the moving periodogram
the spectrum is assumed approximately constant within a tile.

\subsection{One model, two likelihoods}
\label{sec:unified}

Both likelihoods are instances of a single Gaussian-coefficient form. A
time--frequency cell with $R$ real components contributes
\begin{equation}
-\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{n,m}\Big[R\,\Lambda_{nm}
+ \big(\textstyle\sum_{r=1}^{R} c^{(r)2}_{nm}\big)\,e^{-\Lambda_{nm}}\Big],
\label{eq:unified}
\end{equation}
so the WDM coefficient ($R=1$, $\chi^2_1$) and the short-time Fourier
coefficient ($R=2$, real and imaginary parts, $\chi^2_2$, whose squared modulus
is the periodogram) are handled by the \emph{same} likelihood. The exponential
dynamic-Whittle form of Eq.~\eqref{eq:dynamic_whittle} is exactly the $R=2$
marginal of Eq.~\eqref{eq:unified} over the coefficient phase, so the two
subsections above are the same model written for power and for complex
coefficients respectively. Modelling the coefficients rather than the power also
retains phase, so the construction extends to a signal mean
$c\sim\mathcal{N}(h(\bm\theta), S)$, used for the joint inference of
Sec.~\ref{sec:lisa}. The estimator therefore has interchangeable front ends: a
time series is mapped to (time grid, frequency grid, coefficients) by the WDM or
short-time Fourier transform, and the identical whitened P-spline model of
Sec.~\ref{sec:model} fits the surface.

\section{Simulation study}
\label{sec:sim}

We assess statistical performance where the truth is known, fitting the same
P-spline model through both observation models and comparing against the true
surface.

\subsection{Locally stationary process}
Following \citet{tang2023MovingPSD}, we use the locally stationary time-varying
MA(1) process (LS2) with i.i.d.\ standard-normal innovations $\{w_t\}$,
\begin{equation}
X_{t,T} = w_t + 1.1\cos\!\left(1.5 - \cos\!\left(4\pi\tfrac{t}{T}\right)\right) w_{t-1},
\qquad t=1,\ldots,T,
\label{eq:ls2}
\end{equation}
with analytic pointwise PSD $f_0(u,\omega) = 1 + b_1(u)^2 + 2b_1(u)\cos\omega$,
$b_1(u) = 1.1\cos(1.5 - \cos 4\pi u)$. For each realization we fit the model
through the WDM likelihood, Eq.~\eqref{eq:wdm_like}, and through the
moving-periodogram likelihood, Eq.~\eqref{eq:thinned_dynamic_whittle}, and repeat
across a range of observation lengths.

The WDM estimator targets the expected local power $\mathbb{E}[w_{nm}^2]$, which
equals the digital PSD up to a near-constant per-channel normalization,
$\mathbb{E}[w_{nm}^2] = C_m f_0(t_n,f_m)$. We calibrate $C_m$ once from unit
white noise and report the error against both the Monte Carlo $\mathbb{E}[w^2]$
target and the calibrated analytic PSD $C_m f_0$.

\subsection{Metrics}
We summarize point-estimate accuracy with the mean squared error of the log-PSD,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{MSE}_{\log f} =
\frac{1}{T(K+1)}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\sum_{j=0}^{K}
\left(\ln\hat f\!\left(\tfrac{t}{T},\tfrac{j}{K}\right)
- \ln f_0\!\left(\tfrac{t}{T},\tfrac{j}{K}\right)\right)^2,
\label{eq:MSE_log_psd}
\end{equation}
using the posterior mean as the point estimate. We also report the empirical
coverage of the posterior $90\%$ credible intervals and, as a measure of
posterior contraction, the width of the $90\%$ credible interval on $\log S$
averaged over the grid (a scale-free quantity, comparable across
representations). Each configuration is summarized over $100$ independent
realizations; we plot the median and interquartile range across realizations.

\subsection{Results}
Both observation models recover the time-varying surface and contract with the
data (Fig.~\ref{fig:sim_mse_coverage}). Over $100$ realizations at each of four
observation lengths ($n = 576$ to $4608$), the median $\mathrm{MSE}_{\log f}$
falls monotonically with $n$ for both likelihoods, the $90\%$ credible-interval
coverage stays close to nominal, and the credible-interval width on $\log S$
contracts steadily -- the expected Bayesian behaviour. No sampler divergence
occurred in any fit. On this smooth process the moving periodogram is the more
accurate of the two at matched $n$; we return to why the WDM front end is
nonetheless preferable for phase-coherent inference in
Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\section{LISA demonstration}
\label{sec:lisa}

We now demonstrate the framework on a realistic inference problem, where
non-stationarity matters scientifically. We simulate a full year of LISA data
containing the cyclostationary Galactic confusion foreground and one injected
Galactic binary, and perform joint inference of the time-varying noise PSD and
the source. The non-stationarity is not imposed by hand: the confusion power is
modulated by the published annual cyclostationary law of
\citet{digman2022cyclostationary}, derived in the same wavelet domain our
estimator uses.

\subsection{Simulated data}
\label{sec:lisa_data}

The data are a single second-generation TDI channel (TDI-X) sampled at interval
$\Delta t \simeq 200\,\mathrm{s}$ over a one-year observation
($N \simeq 1.6\times10^{5}$ samples, giving a Nyquist frequency of
$2.5\,\mathrm{mHz}$), expressed in fractional-frequency units. They combine an
injected Galactic binary with two independent noise components,
\begin{equation}
d(t) = h(t;\bm\theta) + n_{\mathrm{inst}}(t) + \sqrt{r(u)}\,\,n_{\mathrm{gal}}(t),
\label{eq:lisa_data}
\end{equation}
where $u = t/T_{\mathrm{obs}}\in[0,1]$ is rescaled time. The instrument component
$n_{\mathrm{inst}}$ and the Galactic-confusion component $n_{\mathrm{gal}}$ are
drawn as independent zero-mean coloured Gaussian processes whose one-sided PSDs
are the analytic Robson--Cornish--Liu fits~\citep{robson2019sensitivity}
$S_{\mathrm{inst}}(f)$ and $S_{\mathrm{gal}}(f)$: each is generated in the
frequency domain with
$\tilde n(f_k) \sim \mathcal{CN}\!\big(0,\, S(f_k)/(4\,\Delta f\,\Delta t^2)\big)$
and transformed to the time domain, so the realized one-sided PSD reproduces the
target.

The confusion power is modulated by the cyclostationary annual law of
\citet{digman2022cyclostationary},
\begin{equation}
r(u) = 1 + \sum_{k=1}^{5} A_k \cos\!\big(2\pi k\,u\, T_{\mathrm{obs}}/T_{\mathrm{yr}} - \varphi_k\big),
\label{eq:dc_modulation}
\end{equation}
with the tabulated harmonic coefficients $(A_k,\varphi_k)$ of their Table~1
(A-channel, one-year fit). Physically, $r(u)$ is the antenna pattern sweeping the
anisotropic Galaxy as the constellation orbits the Sun; the second harmonic
dominates, so the confusion power peaks twice per year and varies by a factor
$\simeq 7$ over the year, reproducing the modulation of their Figure~1. The
coefficients average to $\langle r\rangle = 1$ over whole years, so the modulation
redistributes confusion power in time without changing its time average. Because
the two components are independent and $r$ varies slowly relative to a WDM time
bin, the instantaneous one-sided PSD is
\begin{equation}
S(u,f) = S_{\mathrm{inst}}(f) + r(u)\,S_{\mathrm{gal}}(f),
\label{eq:lisa_psd}
\end{equation}
which is exactly the local power the estimator targets.

The binary strain $h(t;\bm\theta)$ is generated with \texttt{jaxGB} in the same
TDI-X, generation-2, fractional-frequency convention as the noise, so the
matched-filter signal-to-noise ratio is physically consistent (we verify it
agrees between the time- and frequency-domain representations). A single
resolvable binary is injected at $f_0 = 1.5\,\mathrm{mHz}$ with an optimal
SNR of $200$, typical of a bright LISA verification binary.

We analyse two scenarios that share the same injected binary and instrument
floor, differing only in the noise stationarity. In Case~1 (stationary noise plus
binary) the confusion modulation is switched off ($r(u)\equiv 1$), so
$S(u,f)=S_{\mathrm{inst}}(f)+S_{\mathrm{gal}}(f)$ is constant in time; this is the
consistency-check case, in which the noise truly is stationary and all
representations should recover the same binary posterior, agreeing with the
standard frequency-domain analysis. In Case~2 (non-stationary noise plus binary)
the full cyclostationary modulation, Eq.~\eqref{eq:dc_modulation}, makes $S(u,f)$
vary in time; this is the discriminating case, in which a stationary noise model
is mis-specified, so we expect it to bias the inference while the non-stationary
WDM and STFT fits remain accurate. Each case is analysed with three
representations -- the traditional frequency-domain Whittle likelihood, the WDM
coefficient likelihood, and the phase-retaining moving short-time-Fourier (STFT)
likelihood (Sec.~\ref{sec:obs}).

\subsection{Blocked Gibbs inference with NUTS sub-kernels}
\label{sec:gibbs}
Because the WDM likelihood admits a signal mean,
$w_{nm}\sim\mathcal{N}(h_{nm}(\bm\theta), S_{nm})$, the source parameters
$\bm\theta$ and the non-stationary noise surface $\bm{\Lambda}$ can be inferred
jointly. Rather than sampling the full state with a single Hamiltonian
trajectory, we use a Metropolis-within-Gibbs scheme that alternates two blocks,
each updated with its own NUTS kernel. The PSD block, conditional on the current
source estimate, subtracts the signal coefficients $h_{nm}(\bm\theta)$ and
updates the whitened noise parameters $(\mathbf{s},\phi_t,\phi_f)$ with NUTS under
the model of Sec.~\ref{sec:model}. The source block, conditional on the current
noise surface $\bm{\Lambda}$, updates the source parameters $\bm\theta$ with a
separate NUTS kernel. Blocking is deliberate: the high-dimensional smooth PSD
coefficients and the low-dimensional, multimodal source parameters have very
different geometries, so each block's NUTS kernel can adapt independently while
the Gibbs sweep couples them. Each sweep warm-starts from the previous one and
discards the first sweeps as burn-in.

\subsection{Stationary baseline}
We compare against a stationary PSD model fitted with the stationary Whittle
likelihood, Eq.~\eqref{eq:whittle}, applied to the \emph{same signal-subtracted
coefficients} that the blocked-Gibbs fit sees (the recovered binary is removed
before both fits). This is the frequency marginal of the model of
Sec.~\ref{sec:model}: a single spectrum $S(f)$, constant in time, represented by a
whitened 1D log-P-spline with the same Gamma-hyperprior smoothing precision. With
$S_m$ shared across time bins the per-channel total power $\sum_n w_{nm}^2$ is
sufficient. The \emph{only} difference from our estimator is the
time-stationarity assumption, so the comparison isolates its effect rather than
conflating it with signal subtraction.

\subsection{Results}

From a single noisy realization spanning one year, the joint fit denoises the raw
$\chi^2_1$ WDM power into a smooth log-PSD surface that matches the Monte Carlo
reference (Fig.~\ref{fig:lisa_surface}). The estimated $\log S(u,f)$ recovers both
the steep instrument spectrum at low frequency and the twice-yearly Galactic
confusion modulation that the raw power buries in noise. Because the coefficient
likelihood retains phase, the Gibbs fit jointly recovers the coherent binary and
the noise surface: with the binary sub-threshold in any single time--frequency
cell, its amplitude is nonetheless recovered to within $\sim\!10\%$ by coherent
summation across the plane ($|A|/|A|_{\mathrm{true}} = 0.90$).

Estimating the four binary parameters $(f_0,\dot f_0, A, \phi_0)$ -- via the
bilinear Cartesian amplitudes $g_c = A\cos\phi_0$, $g_s = A\sin\phi_0$, so the
strain is linear in the sampled amplitudes -- yields posteriors that agree
between the frequency-domain Whittle likelihood and the WDM coefficient
likelihood (Fig.~\ref{fig:gb_corner}). With stationary noise the wavelet domain
therefore costs no information relative to the standard frequency-domain
analysis.

Assuming stationarity, by contrast, mis-estimates the noise
(Fig.~\ref{fig:signal_bias}): in the binary's channel the stationary fit is
necessarily flat in time at the time-averaged level, so it overestimates the
noise when the Galactic confusion is low and underestimates it when the confusion
peaks -- by up to the factor-$\simeq 7$ swing of the modulation -- whereas the
non-stationary Gibbs fit tracks the twice-yearly modulation and contains the true
$S(u,f)$ within its $90\%$ band.

\subsection{Robustness across representations}
\label{sec:lisa_grid}

The same conclusion holds independently of the time--frequency representation. We
analyse the non-stationary noise with all three observation models of
Sec.~\ref{sec:obs}: the WDM coefficient likelihood, the phase-retaining moving
short-time Fourier transform (STFT, the $R=2$ front end), and the power-only
dynamic Whittle (moving periodogram). All three recover the same cyclostationary
modulation of the noise PSD in the binary's channel, tracking the true annual
envelope where a stationary model is flat (Fig.~\ref{fig:rep_compare}, left).
Because the dynamic Whittle discards phase it cannot subtract a coherent template,
so it is applied to the signal-subtracted series purely as a noise-PSD estimator;
the two phase-retaining front ends additionally recover the binary amplitude
consistently ($|A|/|A|_{\mathrm{true}} = 0.90$ and $0.85$;
Fig.~\ref{fig:rep_compare}, right). All three use the identical P-spline model, so
the cost being measured is the stationarity assumption, not the representation.

The few--ten percent residual underestimate of the amplitude reflects a partial
degeneracy between the binary's annual antenna-pattern amplitude modulation and
the cyclostationary confusion in the same channel: a flexible non-stationary noise
floor can absorb a small fraction of the coherent source power. The effect is
absent in the stationary-noise consistency check, where both domains recover the
amplitude without bias (Fig.~\ref{fig:gb_corner}), and we expect it to tighten
with the full nonlinear source model and informative source priors discussed in
Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The framework cleanly separates the statistical model -- the smooth log-PSD
surface and its prior -- from the observation model that relates a
time--frequency data product to that surface. Section~\ref{sec:obs} exercised
this separation with two likelihoods that share the model of Sec.~\ref{sec:model}
verbatim, and any near-orthogonal representation whose coefficients admit a
local-power (Gaussian-coefficient or exponential) likelihood -- multitaper
spectrograms, $Q$-transforms, or other wavelet families -- can be added as a new
front end without changing the model.

The LISA demonstration shows that the choice of stationarity assumption, not the
choice of representation, is what drives the bias: the WDM and STFT front ends
agree with one another and with the frequency-domain analysis where stationarity
holds, and both track the cyclostationary modulation where it does not. Existing
LISA stochastic pipelines model this modulation either with an analytic
cyclostationary template \citep{digman2022cyclostationary, buscicchio2024test,
pozzoli2024bahamas} or with a per-segment confusion amplitude
\citep{rosati2024prototype}; our estimator instead infers the time--frequency
PSD surface non-parametrically, requiring no template for the modulation shape.
This makes the framework a natural component of detector-characterization and
global-fit pipelines \citep{katz2025efficient}, where a mis-specified stationary
noise model otherwise contaminates the recovered source population.

Several extensions follow naturally. The source block can be generalized from the
two linear quadrature amplitudes at fixed frequency to the full parameter vector
$\bm\theta = (f_0, \dot f_0, A, \ldots)$ with a nonlinear, multimodal frequency
search, leaving the blocked structure unchanged. Beyond a single source, the same
coefficient likelihood supports multi-source and multi-channel joint fits, and
relaxing the diagonal approximation to a correlated-coefficient likelihood would
account for the residual nearest-neighbour correlations left by a finitely
orthogonal transform. Finally, transient and time-localized structure is where
critically-sampled wavelets should outperform the moving periodogram, a regime we
leave to future work.
