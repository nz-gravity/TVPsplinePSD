\begin{abstract}
We introduce a unified Bayesian framework for estimating smoothly
time-varying power spectral densities (PSDs) from time--frequency data. The
core of the framework is a single statistical model: the log-PSD surface
$\log S(t,f)$ is represented by a tensor-product P-spline with an anisotropic
roughness prior, sampled in a non-centered (whitened) parameterization that
removes the hierarchical funnel obstructing Hamiltonian Monte Carlo. The key
design principle is that this model is \emph{independent of the underlying
time--frequency representation}: the same prior and the same smooth surface can
be combined with different observation models. We develop two such likelihoods
-- one for moving-periodogram ordinates and one for Wilson--Daubechies--Meyer
(WDM) wavelet coefficients -- and show that they plug into the identical PSD
model. In a simulation study on a locally stationary process with known truth,
both observation models recover the time-varying surface with near-nominal
credible intervals across a range of observation lengths. We then demonstrate
the framework on a realistic LISA inference problem with seasonally modulated
Galactic confusion noise and an injected Galactic binary, where a Gibbs-style
scheme alternates between PSD estimation and source parameter estimation, and
show that assuming stationarity biases both the recovered noise PSD and the
source parameters. The result is a modular framework, applicable to any
time--frequency representation whose coefficients admit a local-power
likelihood.
\end{abstract}

\paragraph{Time--frequency representations.}
A non-stationary process is naturally described in a joint time--frequency
representation. Two complementary choices are (i) the moving / short-time
periodogram, which slides a window along the series, and (ii) critically-sampled
wavelet transforms such as the WDM transform \citep{cornish2020Wavelets}, which
tile the plane into a near-orthogonal grid. Each produces a different data
product, but both estimate the same underlying object: a slowly varying PSD
surface.

\paragraph{Smooth PSD evolution.}
When the spectrum evolves smoothly in time and frequency, the estimation problem
becomes one of fitting a smooth bivariate surface to noisy local-power
observations -- a regression problem to which penalized splines are well suited.

confusion noise and an injected binary, where assuming stationarity introduces

\section{Bayesian model}
\label{sec:model}

This section defines the statistical model for the time-varying PSD. It is
deliberately stated \emph{independently of the data representation}: the surface,
prior, and inference machinery below are shared by every observation model in
Section~\ref{sec:obs}.

\subsection{Time-varying PSD model}
\label{sec:tvpsd}

Let $S(t,f) > 0$ denote the power spectral density at time $t$ and frequency
$f$, assumed to vary smoothly in both arguments. We model its logarithm,
\begin{equation}
\Lambda(t,f) \;=\; \log S(t,f),
\end{equation}
working on the log scale so that the surface is unconstrained and the
multiplicative dynamic range of the PSD becomes additive. The estimand is the
function $\Lambda(t,f)$ evaluated on a time--frequency grid; the specific grid
$(t_n, f_m)$ is supplied by the chosen representation in Section~\ref{sec:obs},
but the model below does not depend on how that grid arises.

\subsection{Tensor-product P-spline representation}
\label{sec:pspline}

\paragraph{Basis expansion.}
We represent the log-PSD surface as a tensor-product P-spline
\citep{eilers2003multivariate, wood2017generalized},
\begin{equation}
\Lambda(t_i, f_j) = \sum_{k=1}^{K_t}\sum_{l=1}^{K_f} w_{kl}\,
B_k^{(t)}(t_i)\, B_l^{(f)}(f_j),
\end{equation}
where $B_k^{(t)}$ and $B_l^{(f)}$ are B-spline bases of degree $d_t, d_f$ in
time and frequency, $K_t, K_f$ are the corresponding basis dimensions, and
$w_{kl}$ are the spline coefficients. In matrix form, with
$\bm{\Lambda}\in\mathbb{R}^{n_t\times n_f}$ the surface on the grid,
$\mathbf{B}_t\in\mathbb{R}^{n_t\times K_t}$, $\mathbf{B}_f\in\mathbb{R}^{n_f\times K_f}$
the basis matrices, and $\mathbf{W}\in\mathbb{R}^{K_t\times K_f}$ the coefficient
matrix,
\begin{equation}
\bm{\Lambda} = \mathbf{B}_t\,\mathbf{W}\,\mathbf{B}_f^\top .
\end{equation}
Equivalently, with $\mathbf{w} = \mathrm{vec}(\mathbf{W})$,
$\mathrm{vec}(\bm{\Lambda}) = (\mathbf{B}_f \otimes \mathbf{B}_t)\,\mathbf{w}$
\citep{boer2023tensor}; in practice we never form the Kronecker design and
evaluate the dense product directly.

\paragraph{Penalties.}
Smoothness is imposed by difference penalties in each direction. With $\mathbf{P}_t$
and $\mathbf{P}_f$ the marginal difference-penalty matrices (orders $m_t, m_f$),
the anisotropic penalty on $\mathbf{w}$ has the Kronecker-sum structure
\citep{wood2017generalized}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Q}(\phi_t,\phi_f)
= \phi_t\,(\mathbf{I}_{K_f}\otimes\mathbf{P}_t)
+ \phi_f\,(\mathbf{P}_f\otimes\mathbf{I}_{K_t}),
\label{eq:penalty}
\end{equation}
with $\phi_t, \phi_f > 0$ the time and frequency smoothing precisions.

\paragraph{Smoothing prior.}
The penalty defines a Gaussian roughness prior on the coefficients,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{w}\mid\phi_t,\phi_f \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mathbf{0},\,\mathbf{Q}^{-1}\right),
\label{eq:prior}
\end{equation}
so that all smoothing information is carried by the two penalties and the two
precisions \citep{lang2004bayesian,maturana2021bayesian,boer2023tensor}.

\paragraph{Whitened (non-centered) parameterization.}
Sampling $\mathbf{w}$ jointly with $(\phi_t,\phi_f)$ produces the classic
hierarchical funnel: the conditional scale of $\mathbf{w}$ collapses as the
precisions grow, which during gradient-based MCMC manifests as vanishing
gradients and runs that saturate the maximum tree depth. We remove this by
sampling in the eigenbasis of the marginal penalties. With the eigendecompositions
$\mathbf{P}_t = \mathbf{U}_t\,\mathrm{diag}(\lambda^t)\,\mathbf{U}_t^\top$ and
$\mathbf{P}_f = \mathbf{U}_f\,\mathrm{diag}(\lambda^f)\,\mathbf{U}_f^\top$, the
two Kronecker terms are simultaneously diagonalized by $\mathbf{U}_f\otimes\mathbf{U}_t$,
so $\mathbf{Q}$ has eigenvalues $d_{ab} = \phi_t\lambda^t_a + \phi_f\lambda^f_b$.
We sample standard-normal eigen-coefficients $s_{ab}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and set
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{W} = \mathbf{U}_t\,\mathbf{Z}\,\mathbf{U}_f^\top,
\qquad
Z_{ab} = \frac{s_{ab}}{\sqrt{d_{ab}}},
\label{eq:whitening}
\end{equation}
which reproduces $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{Q}^{-1})$ exactly while making
$s$ \emph{a priori} independent of $(\phi_t,\phi_f)$. The change of variables
absorbs the $|\mathbf{Q}|^{1/2}$ normalization, so no explicit penalty
determinant is needed. The shared null space of the two penalties (bilinear
polynomials in $t,f$, where $d_{ab}=0$) carries no smoothing information and is
assigned a fixed weak precision $\tau_0$.

\paragraph{Hyperpriors.}
Each smoothing precision is given a Gamma hyperprior
\citep{maturana2021bayesian},
\begin{equation}
\phi_t,\ \phi_f \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha_\phi, \beta_\phi),
\end{equation}
sampled on the unconstrained log scale, with the Gamma density and its
log-Jacobian supplied as a factor. \todo{Justify the default
$(\alpha_\phi, \beta_\phi)$; relate to penalised-complexity priors
\citep{simpson2017penalising} if used.}

\subsection{Posterior inference}
\label{sec:inference}

Let $\mathcal{L}(\bm{\Lambda})$ denote the observation-model log-likelihood
(Section~\ref{sec:obs}), which depends on the data only through the surface
$\bm{\Lambda}$. The joint posterior over the whitened eigen-coefficients and the
smoothing precisions is
\begin{equation}
p(\mathbf{s}, \phi_t, \phi_f \mid \text{data})
\;\propto\;
\exp\!\big(\mathcal{L}(\bm{\Lambda}(\mathbf{s},\phi_t,\phi_f))\big)\,
p(\mathbf{s})\,p(\phi_t)\,p(\phi_f),
\end{equation}
with $p(\mathbf{s}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I})$ and $\bm{\Lambda}$
obtained from $(\mathbf{s},\phi_t,\phi_f)$ through the deterministic whitening
map~\eqref{eq:whitening}; no explicit penalty factor appears.

\caption{Whitened tensor-product log-P-spline PSD model (one likelihood

\begin{algorithmic}
\STATE \textbf{Sample smoothing precisions (log scale):}
$\log\phi_t,\ \log\phi_f$, with $\phi_t,\phi_f\sim\mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha_\phi,\beta_\phi)$

\section{Observation models}
\label{sec:obs}

The model of Section~\ref{sec:model} specifies a prior over the smooth surface
$\bm{\Lambda} = \log\mathbf{S}$. To complete the posterior we need a likelihood
that links a time--frequency data product to this surface. This section gives
two such likelihoods. \emph{The Bayesian PSD model is unchanged in both cases;
only the likelihood $\mathcal{L}(\bm{\Lambda})$ differs.}

\subsection{Moving periodogram likelihood}
\label{sec:movperiod}

\paragraph{Moving windows and local periodograms.}
A short window of length $2m+1$ is slid along the series and a local periodogram
is formed at each step \citep{tang2023MovingPSD}.

\begin{definition}[Moving periodogram ordinates]
The \emph{moving periodogram ordinates} $\mathrm{MI}_t$ (of order $m$) at time
point $t = 1,\ldots,T$ of $X_{t-m+1},\ldots,X_{t+m}$ are
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{MI}_t = \frac{1}{2\pi(2m+1)}
\left|\sum_{\nu=0}^{2m} X_{\nu+t-m}\,
\exp\!\left(-i\pi\nu\,\lambda_{\mathrm{mod}(t)}\right)\right|^2,
\label{eq:MI}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_j = \tfrac{2j}{2m+1}$, $j=1,\ldots,m$, are the Fourier
frequencies and $\mathrm{mod}(t) = 1 + ((t-1)\bmod m)$.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Approximate likelihood.}
For a locally stationary process the moving periodogram ordinates are
approximately independent and exponentially distributed with mean $S$, giving
the dynamic Whittle likelihood.

\begin{definition}[Dynamic Whittle likelihood]
\begin{equation}
L^{(1)}_{\mathrm{DW}}(S)
= \prod_{t=1}^{T}
\frac{1}{S(t/T,\lambda_{\mathrm{mod}(t)})}
\exp\!\left(-\frac{\mathrm{MI}_t}{S(t/T,\lambda_{\mathrm{mod}(t)})}\right).
\label{eq:dynamic_whittle}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

To reduce the dependence induced by overlapping windows, a thinned variant
retains a zigzag subset of ordinates \citep{tang2023MovingPSD}.

\begin{definition}[Thinned dynamic Whittle likelihood]
With thinning factor $i$,
\begin{equation}
L^{(i)}_{\mathrm{DW}}(S)
= \prod_{l=1}^{B_i}\prod_{j=1}^{m}
\frac{1}{S(u_{j,l,i},\lambda_j)}
\exp\!\left(-\frac{\mathrm{MI}(u_{j,l,i},\lambda_j)}{S(u_{j,l,i},\lambda_j)}\right),
\label{eq:thinned_dynamic_whittle}
\end{equation}
where $B_i = \lfloor (T-m)/(im)\rfloor$ and $u_{j,l,i} = (i(l-1)m+j)/T$.
\end{definition}

In the notation of Section~\ref{sec:model}, $\mathcal{L}(\bm{\Lambda}) =
\log L^{(i)}_{\mathrm{DW}}$ with $S = e^{\Lambda}$ evaluated at the ordinate
locations.

\paragraph{Assumptions.}
The dynamic Whittle treats the (thinned) ordinates as independent and
exponential; this is exact only asymptotically and requires the spectrum to be
approximately constant across a window. Thinning trades data for reduced
inter-ordinate correlation. The power periodogram discards phase.

\subsection{WDM likelihood}
\label{sec:wdm}

\paragraph{WDM coefficients.}
The Wilson--Daubechies--Meyer (WDM) transform \citep{cornish2020Wavelets} tiles
the data into a near-orthogonal time--frequency grid of coefficients $w_{nm}$,
where $n$ indexes WDM time bins and $m$ frequency channels.

\paragraph{Coefficient distribution.}
For a locally stationary process the diagonal approximation models each pixel as
a zero-mean Gaussian whose variance is the local evolutionary power,
\begin{equation}
w_{nm}\sim\mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\,S_{nm}\right),
\qquad S_{nm} = S(t_n, f_m).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Likelihood.}
The resulting log-likelihood, with $\bm{\Lambda} = \log\mathbf{S}$, is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(\bm{\Lambda})
= -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{n,m}
\left[\log(2\pi) + \Lambda_{nm} + w_{nm}^2\,e^{-\Lambda_{nm}}\right].
\label{eq:wdm_like}
\end{equation}
Each pixel contributes a single $\chi^2_1$ observation; the smoothing prior of
Section~\ref{sec:pspline} supplies the regularization that makes
single-realization estimation feasible.

\paragraph{Assumptions.}
The likelihood treats the WDM coefficients as independent, exact only for a
perfectly orthogonal transform; residual nearest-neighbour time correlation
remains. As with the moving periodogram, the spectrum is assumed approximately
constant within a tile.

\subsection{One model, two likelihoods}
\label{sec:unified}

Both likelihoods are instances of a single Gaussian-coefficient form. A
time--frequency cell with $R$ real components contributes
\begin{equation}
-\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{n,m}\Big[R\,\Lambda_{nm}
+ \big(\textstyle\sum_{r=1}^{R} c^{(r)2}_{nm}\big)\,e^{-\Lambda_{nm}}\Big],
\end{equation}
so the WDM coefficient ($R=1$, $\chi^2_1$) and the short-time Fourier
coefficient ($R=2$, real and imaginary parts, $\chi^2_2$, whose squared modulus
is the periodogram) are handled by the \emph{same} likelihood. Modelling the
coefficients rather than the power also retains phase, so the construction
extends to a signal mean $c\sim\mathcal{N}(h(\bm\theta), S)$, used for the joint
inference of Section~\ref{sec:lisa}. The estimator therefore has interchangeable
front ends: a time series is mapped to (time grid, frequency grid, coefficients)
by the WDM or short-time Fourier transform, and the identical whitened P-spline
model of Section~\ref{sec:model} fits the surface.

\section{Simulation study}
\label{sec:sim}

We assess statistical performance where the truth is known, fitting the same
P-spline model through both observation models and comparing against the true
surface.

\subsection{Locally stationary process (LS2)}
Following \citet{tang2023MovingPSD}, we use the locally stationary time-varying
MA(1) process with i.i.d.\ standard-normal innovations $\{w_t\}$,
\begin{equation}
\text{LS2:}\quad X_{t,T} = w_t + 1.1\cos\!\left(1.5 - \cos\!\left(4\pi\tfrac{t}{T}\right)\right) w_{t-1},
\qquad t=1,\ldots,T,
\label{eq:ls2}
\end{equation}
with analytic pointwise PSD $f_0(u,\omega) = 1 + b_1(u)^2 + 2b_1(u)\cos\omega$,
$b_1(u) = 1.1\cos(1.5 - \cos 4\pi u)$. For each realization we fit the model
through the WDM likelihood~\eqref{eq:wdm_like} and through the moving-periodogram
likelihood~\eqref{eq:thinned_dynamic_whittle}, and repeat across a range of
observation lengths.

\paragraph{Calibration note (WDM target).}
The WDM estimator targets the expected local power $\mathbb{E}[w_{nm}^2]$, which
equals the digital PSD up to a near-constant per-channel normalization,
$\mathbb{E}[w_{nm}^2] = C_m f_0(t_n,f_m)$. We calibrate $C_m$ once from unit
white noise and report the error against both the Monte Carlo $\mathbb{E}[w^2]$
target and the calibrated analytic PSD $C_m f_0$.

\subsection{Metrics}
We summarize point-estimate accuracy with the mean squared error of the log-PSD,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{MSE}_{\log f} =
\frac{1}{T(K+1)}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\sum_{j=0}^{K}
\left(\ln\hat f\!\left(\tfrac{t}{T},\tfrac{j}{K}\right)
- \ln f_0\!\left(\tfrac{t}{T},\tfrac{j}{K}\right)\right)^2,
\label{eq:MSE_log_psd}
\end{equation}
using the posterior mean as the point estimate, and we report the empirical
coverage of the posterior $90\%$ credible intervals. Each reported value is
averaged over approximately $20$ independent realizations.

\subsection{Results}
Both observation models recover the time-varying surface and contract with the
data. Over $20$ realizations at each of four observation lengths
($n = 576$ to $4608$), the median $\mathrm{MSE}_{\log f}$ falls monotonically
with $n$ for both likelihoods (Fig.~\ref{fig:sim_mse_coverage}, upper) -- from
$0.21$ to $0.04$ for WDM and from $0.13$ to $0.03$ for the moving periodogram --
and the $90\%$ credible-interval coverage stays close to nominal (lower panel;
WDM $0.87$--$0.90$, moving periodogram $0.79$--$0.89$, the latter drifting
slightly anti-conservative at large $n$ from residual overlap between windows).
No sampler divergence occurred in any of the $160$ fits. On this smooth process
the moving periodogram is the more accurate of the two at matched $n$; we return
to why the WDM front end is nonetheless preferable for phase-coherent inference
in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\section{LISA demonstration}
\label{sec:lisa}

We now demonstrate the framework on a realistic inference problem, where
non-stationarity matters scientifically. We simulate LISA data containing an
evolving Galactic confusion foreground and one injected Galactic binary, and
perform joint inference of the noise PSD and the source.

\subsection{Simulated data}
We simulate independent instrument and Galactic-confusion components and modulate
the confusion amplitude seasonally,
\begin{equation}
x(t) = x_{\mathrm{inst}}(t) + m(u)\,x_{\mathrm{gal}}(t),
\qquad
S(u,f) = S_{\mathrm{inst}}(f) + m(u)^2\,S_{\mathrm{gal}}(f),
\end{equation}
with $u = t/T_{\mathrm{obs}}$ the rescaled time. The instrument and confusion
PSDs $S_{\mathrm{inst}}(f)$ and $S_{\mathrm{gal}}(f)$ are the analytic
Robson--Cornish--Liu fits~\citep{cornish2020Wavelets}, and $m(u)$ is a
slowly varying seasonal envelope (a raised cosine of the antenna-pattern cycle)
normalized to $\langle m^2\rangle = 1$ so that the modulation redistributes
confusion power in time without changing its time average. Both noise components
are drawn as independent coloured Gaussian realizations matching their one-sided
PSDs; because they are independent and $m$ varies slowly, $S(u,f)$ is exactly the
WDM local power the estimator targets.

For a physically consistent signal-plus-noise problem the binary and the noise
share a single convention. The instrument noise PSD is taken from
\texttt{lisatools} (model \texttt{scirdv1}) and the binary waveform from
\texttt{jaxGB}, both in the TDI-X channel, TDI generation~2, and fractional-
frequency units; mixing TDI generations would mis-scale the signal-to-noise ratio
by a large frequency-dependent factor. We verify the convention by recovering the
same matched-filter SNR from the time-domain and frequency-domain
representations. A single resolvable binary is injected at $f_0 = 1.5\,\mathrm{mHz}$
(zero $\dot f_0$) at an SNR of order ten against the confusion-dominated floor.

\subsection{Blocked Gibbs inference with NUTS sub-kernels}
\label{sec:gibbs}
Because the WDM likelihood admits a signal mean,
$w_{nm}\sim\mathcal{N}(h_{nm}(\bm\theta), S_{nm})$, the source parameters
$\bm\theta$ and the non-stationary noise surface $\bm{\Lambda}$ can be inferred
jointly. Rather than sampling the full state with a single Hamiltonian
trajectory, we use a Metropolis-within-Gibbs scheme that alternates two blocks,
\emph{each updated with its own NUTS kernel}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{PSD block:} conditional on the current source estimate, subtract
the signal coefficients $h_{nm}(\bm\theta)$ and update the whitened noise
parameters $(\mathbf{s},\phi_t,\phi_f)$ with NUTS under the model of
Section~\ref{sec:model};
\item \textbf{Source block:} conditional on the current noise surface
$\bm{\Lambda}$, update the source parameters $\bm\theta$ with a separate NUTS
kernel.
\end{enumerate}
Blocking is deliberate: the smooth, high-dimensional PSD coefficients and the
low-dimensional, strongly non-Gaussian (multimodal in frequency) source
parameters have very different posterior geometries, so a single joint sampler
must compromise on one shared mass matrix and step size. Updating each block with
its own adapted NUTS kernel lets the two adapt independently while the Gibbs
sweep couples them through the conditioning.
This scheme is implemented in \texttt{run\_gibbs\_signal\_noise\_mcmc}: each
sweep runs a short, warm-started NUTS update of the noise block on the residual
coefficients followed by a NUTS update of the amplitude block at the fixed noise
surface, with the first sweeps discarded as burn-in.

\subsection{Stationary baseline}
We compare against a stationary PSD model fitted with the stationary Whittle
likelihood, holding the source model fixed
(\texttt{run\_stationary\_psd\_mcmc}). This is the frequency marginal of the model
of Section~\ref{sec:model}: a single spectrum $S(f)$, constant in time,
represented by a whitened 1D log-P-spline with the same Gamma-hyperprior
smoothing precision. With $S_m$ shared across time bins the per-channel total
power $\sum_n w_{nm}^2$ is sufficient. The \emph{only} difference from our
estimator is the time-stationarity assumption, which isolates its effect.

\subsection{Results}

\paragraph{Recovered noise surface.}
From a single noisy realization, the joint fit denoises the raw $\chi^2_1$ WDM
power into a smooth log-PSD surface that matches the Monte Carlo reference
(Fig.~\ref{fig:lisa_surface}). The estimated $\log S(u,f)$ recovers both the
steep instrument spectrum at low frequency and the time-varying Galactic
confusion that the raw power buries in noise.

\paragraph{Signal/noise separation.}
The coefficient likelihood retains phase, so the Gibbs fit separates the data into
a coherent binary and a noise surface (Fig.~\ref{fig:signal_decomp}): the
recovered signal is a sharp line in the WDM plane and the residual is consistent
with noise. The injected amplitude is recovered ($|A| \approx 0.92$ against an
injected $1.0$) with no sampler divergences.

\paragraph{Binary posteriors.}
Estimating the four binary parameters $(f_0,\dot f_0, A, \phi_0)$ -- via the
bilinear Cartesian amplitudes $g_c = A\cos\phi_0$, $g_s = A\sin\phi_0$, so the
strain is linear in the sampled amplitudes -- yields posteriors that agree
between the frequency-domain Whittle likelihood and the WDM coefficient
likelihood (Fig.~\ref{fig:gb_corner}). With stationary noise the wavelet domain
therefore costs no information relative to the standard frequency-domain analysis.

\paragraph{Cost of assuming stationarity.}
Assuming stationarity biases the inferred noise (Fig.~\ref{fig:signal_bias}): in
the binary's channel the stationary fit is necessarily flat in time and sits above
the truth -- it absorbs the seasonally modulated confusion power -- whereas the
non-stationary Gibbs fit tracks the annual modulation and contains the true
$S(u,f)$ within its $90\%$ band.

\subsection{Robustness across representations}
\label{sec:lisa_grid}

The same conclusion holds independently of the time--frequency representation. On
the non-stationary noise, both blocked-Gibbs fits -- the WDM transform and the
phase-retaining moving short-time Fourier transform (STFT, the $R=2$ front end;
the power-only moving periodogram cannot subtract a coherent template) -- track
the modulated PSD in the binary's channel and recover the injected amplitude
consistently ($|A|/|A|_{\mathrm{true}} = 0.92$ and $0.91$, no divergences), while
the stationary model is biased (Fig.~\ref{fig:rep_compare}). The STFT leg is
fitted with the identical P-spline model through
\texttt{run\_gibbs\_stft\_signal\_noise\_mcmc}, so the cost being measured is the
stationarity assumption, not the representation.

surface and its prior) from the observation model (how a time--frequency data

with two likelihoods that share the model of Section~\ref{sec:model} verbatim.
